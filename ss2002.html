SS2002 – Woe Onto Those Who Spit on the Fear Generation…

Raf Simons’ Spring/Summer 2002 collection, titled “Woe Onto Those Who Spit on the Fear Generation… The Wind Will Blow It Back”, stands as a hauntingly prescient moment in fashion history. Presented in January 2001—nine months before 9/11 and six months before the G8 protests—the show featured models shrouded in ski masks, clutching torches, and walking barefoot through a Parisian lycée. Their draped, oversized white garments interrupted by bursts of red, yellow, and black symbolized both vulnerability and defiance, while slogans like “Be Pure, Be Vigilant, Behave” and “We Are Ready and Willing to Ignite, Just Born Too Late” channeled a generation’s simmering unrest.

Central to this collection was the Kollaps hoodie (image below), a collaboration with artist Peter de Potter, whose dripping-letter graphics became synonymous with Simons’ critique of societal tension. The now-iconic “Ready to Ignite” tee, paired with baggy trousers and draped coats, embodied a rebellious idealism that predated the post-9/11 era’s claustrophobic masculinity. Simons later described the show as a call to “protesters of war and peacekeepers alike,” rejecting misinterpretations of it as glamorizing terror. Instead, the white palette symbolized hope amid chaos, while masked models evoked anonymity as both a shield and a provocation.

“Clothing can carry the weight of the times – it’s a language of resistance.”
— Raf Simons, SS2002 interview

The collection’s cultural resonance only grew with time. Its release of limited graphic tees and crewneVouscks, like the “ Ne Pouvez Plus Ignorer” sweatshirt, became archival grails, fetching upwards of $25,000 today. A signed monograph documenting the show, published in 2002, further cemented its legacy as a manifesto for youth disillusionment, with Simons later reflecting that its timing—on the cusp of global trauma—nearly jeopardized his career.

By merging militant symbolism with minimalist tailoring, Simons not only anticipated the anxieties of the 2000s but redefined fashion’s role as a mirror to societal fractures. The collection remains a touchstone for its raw portrayal of generational fear, proving that clothing can articulate what words often cannot.
